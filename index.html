Real-time American Sign Language (ASL) Recognition üßè

Ever since I noticed the sign language alphabet on the last few pages of my student handbook sometime in fourth grade, learning ASL has been a lifelong goal of mine. About a decade later, I've finally come back to learn it. In a eureka moment, I realized that I could develop two skills at once: ASL and ML.

ASL translation has been picking up speed over the past few years, but the technology is still underdeveloped compared to written/spoken language translation. Google has made perhaps the most significant progress, but given the fact that ASL involves subtle and rapid finger movement, body movement, facial expressions, and its own rules of grammar, it's by no means a closed problem. While I wouldn't claim to have the means to compete with a team of PhD-holding researchers at Google, I'm approaching the problem nonetheless because I believe ASL translation deserves more research and hacking.

At the moment I'm using Google's MediaPipe to perform the hand landmark detection, and at the moment I'm only training models on static, one-hand signs, like 24 of the 26 letters (J and Z require motion). I experimented with both a neural network and a support vector classifier, and while I expected the SVC to outperform the NN due to the way SVMs construct their decision boundaries, the SVC was actually more prone to confuse letters (e.g. E and O). That being said, I'll come back to the SVC to determine if further optimization could improve the results.

The next big step is to add a temporal aspect to the classification. This will be a huge shift, so I'll have to think very carefully about the architecture. A key issue would be determining when one sign stops and the next begins; native signers transition seamlessly between signs, much like we don't pause between words when we speak. So, I should look into how speech-to-text translation works for insight. I'll keep this page updated!

Metaschedule üóìÔ∏è GitHub Website

Metaschedule started as a frustration; namely, a frustration with how difficult it was to plan courses at my college consortium. There already exists a tool for course planning called Hyperschedule, but it requires users to specify which section of a course they wanted to take. For many students (myself included), knowing which sections to take to avoid conflicts is the problem, so Hyperschedule isn't particularly useful. If I had a nickel for every time my course registration plan was torn asunder by courses filling up at the last moment, I would have 20 cents.

Not enough to pay for laundry, but enough for me to make this scheduler tool that allows users to add courses they're interested in and explore the different non-conflicting permutations of those courses' sections, as well as provide options for filtering specific sections and adding custom commitments.

The course data comes from an API endpoint provided by Hyperschedule, helpfully enough, so credit where credit is due :)

Developing Metaschedule taught me a lot about web development (and equally as much about what I still have to learn) but, more importantly, it taught me that I can learn new and exciting skills by jumping in headfirst, asking around for help, and getting feedback on where to improve.

Fun fact: at the bottom of the HTML file is an ASCII representation of my roommate.

P-ai && Artificially Generated Jazz üé∑ Article GitHub Soundcloud

I joined P-ai as a freshman, the year it was founded. Its vision was to become the incubator for all things machine learning at the consortium, and it's done exactly that. In my first year, I was accepted to a project team performing sentiment analysis on Tweets about #WeWork and I took on a second project in the spring to help a senior with his research on computationally lightweight facial recognition.

Coming into my freshman year, I had no experience with machine learning at all, which is why I spent a disproportionate amount of my time on these projects, reading technical articles, attending machine learning workshops after class, and working on side projects to get myself up to speed.

After my first year, I was invited to join the leadership team. In a promising new organization with over 100 members, joining the leadership has been both fast-paced and exciting, letting me take on a wide number of responsibilities like hosting ML workshops, helping organize a career panel, overseeing our project teams, and finding corporate partners.

Most personally interesting to me, I got to lead a project team of my own in generating artificial jazz! Dubbed p-jazz (to match the theme), my team and I have been working on two different avenues of training models to replicate jazz piano from MIDI files: splitting music into chunks (phrases) and training an encoder-decoder model to "translate" from one phrase to the next, and finetuning GPT-2 to replicate the MIDI in text form.

The project is still ongoing, but our GitHub is here. We've also had some preliminary results (music), but we're actively working on ideas for improvements, like training harmony and melody in tandem, and using transfer learning from classical music to jazz. In any case, ML + music is a dream come true for me.
